Unwound

A Story of Maturity

Tumwater's Unwound emerged in 1991 an unknown quantity on the face of rock, remaining shrouded in obscurity until their 2002 breakup. Despite this, the legacy of their work has instilled a lasting impact in the American underground, demonstrating the depth a band can achieve with little more than genuine passion.

Though originally set firmly in a growing post-hardcore scene, Unwound developed into a band unbound by labels along a journey of exploration and experimentation.

By examining their discography, an understanding can be gained on the nature of growth - from pain to clarity.

There they sat—one young woman and two young men, their clothes drab and their hair unkempt—huddled together as if for warmth. All around them, people were talking, joking, laughing. Not these three. They clutched half-eaten burritos like starving children, whispering among themselves.

I walked over. I said something.

They stopped and stared at me. Their faces were wary and bewildered, as if they were zoo animals and I had rattled the bars of their cage.

"Thanks," said one of the young men. I never knew someone could mumble and snarl in one syllable. There was no gratitude in the word—only a sardonic loathing that seemed aimed at everything and everyone in the room, maybe even the young man who said it. Then he turned back to his two comrades and resumed whispering. I stood there for a few seconds before I realized I had been dismissed.

All I'd said was, "I really love your band."
With a band like Unwound, its influence and successors far outshine its own story. Which is a shame, because Unwound synthesized all that was exciting about Olympia and music in the Pacific Northwest.

Its music dark and often experimental; it had pop riffs that grew out of murkiness only to disappear again; its songs gave you glimmers of light but never flooded you with sun; there was angst but not brutality; it possessed an uneven wilderness, which is all you'd ever want from music, something unexpected emerging from what we already know.
Annexed to the less mainstream channels of indie releases, low-budget touring, and a circuit of basement and underground shows, Unwound still managed to create some of the more influential and lasting work of their era.
Just know that if you like contemporary music even remotely associated with artsy, experimental, sorta fucked up rock music, you like a band that sounds at least a little bit like Unwound. You enjoy a band greatly influenced by them, or at the very least has cribbed a few of their tricks.

Self-Titled

Recorded 1992, Released 1995

  • Justin Trosper

  • Vern Rumsey

  • Brandt Sandeno

Unwound's first, self-titled album reveals the band at their most raw. The only album featuring the band's first drummer, Brandt Sandeno, Unwound takes the formula of their hardcore predecessors to darker sonic territories.

Unwound combines '80s hardcore resounding guitar lines on tracks such as "Kid Gone," "You Bite My Tongue," and "Warmth" with "Stuck in The Middle of Nowhere Again," introducing the slower, darker, more melodic progressions the band would later explore, develop, and perfect.

From Justin Trosper's frenzied yelling, metallic guitar scratching, grating noise, driving 8th note guitar lines, the band consistently plants the listening in an anxious soundscape. This chaos culminates in Unwound's closer, "Kandy Korn Rituals", seeing the band at their most explosive and dissonant with Trosper's screams reaching a disturbing desperation.

The trio then decided to do, in concept, a very punk rock thing: they each took on an instrument they did not know how to play. Sandeno played drums, Trosper the guitar, and Rumsey the bass.
Taking leads from forebears such as Black Flag, Unwound's roots lay in the break-neck hardcore sound, which rose from the ashes of punk's brief first wave with a vengeance via bands such as Minor Threat, Minutemen, and Dead Kennedys, who took the spartan fundamentals of guitar, bass, and drums to a terminal velocity.

By degrees, we were putting the kibosh on the thinly veiled teenage stupidity Giant Henry had us engaged in and attempting something with more intent and immediacy.

This became Unwound.

Fake Train

Released 1993

  • Justin Trosper

  • Vern Rumsey

  • Sara Lund

Olympia-based independent label Kill Rock Stars, undersaw a shift from producing exclusively spoken word in 1993 after its owner, Slim Moon, went to see a new local band live - Unwound. Fake Train would be Kill Rock Stars' first music album. Drowned in distortion and waves of feedback, Fake Train soon marked itself as a greatly influential album in the American underground.

Continuing the intensity of their unreleased self-titled LP, Fake Train shows an Unwound beginning to sprout, introducing the angular rythmns and melodic expanses that would come to serve as the bedrock of the band's later work.

Fake Train matches its frantic, unrelenting "Lucky Acid" with songs that, while still heavy, feature extended instrumental interludes where Unwound's guitars let free to chart a course across an unknown landscape of distortion and texture, bound by an eerie melody. Among these introspective expanses arrives "Were, Are, and Was or Is," a mouthful of a song that transports the listener from our cold reality to a five minute and forty four second melodic, guitar-driven, purely-instrumental world.

By the time of Fake Train's release in 1993 there was a well established, nation-wide scene of post-hardcore bands, including Fugazi, Husker Dü, Slint, and The Jesus Lizard, who were striving to expand the sonic boundaries of hardcore whilst maintaining it's mangled aesthetic.

This new approach allowed for a greater complexity of melody and rhythm, the creation of slow-burning compositions, and the use of spatialised effects such as echo and reverb.
With a more counterintuitive percussionist behind the kit, Unwound's sound was suddenly roomier, dubbier and more elastic.
The first time Justin, Vern, and I played together, I knew it was the right fit. From then on, we knit our heads together to create a whole much greater than the sum of its parts.

The culture we created in our tiny universe could be alienating to the casual observer, but once you were in, you were a member for life.

New Plastic

Ideas

Released 1994

  • Justin Trosper

  • Vern Rumsey

  • Sara Lund

In a few months, deep into a grueling tour schedule, Unwound delivered their sophmore 1994 release, New Plastic Ideas. Despite the uphill battle Unwound was faced with, the band further expanded their unique mix of angular rythmns and dream-like interludes from a mere addition to a defining sonic trait.

With Sara Lund's drumming developing a distinct, fill-driven style, the Tumwater trio pressed headfirst into noise rock with irregularity at heart, evidenced immediately by their explosive album opener, "Entirely Different Matters."

The middle stretch of New Plastic Ideas introduces "Abstraktions," a true 7 minute long instrumental successor to Fake Train's "Were, Are, and Was or Is", descending into a desolate abyss rather than blissful entropy. Providing a helping hand out of the previous track's dark, vast soundscape "All Souls Day" pulls the listener straight back into Unwound's juxtaposing uncompromising noise.

In an album littered with bleak tones and jaded lyrics, New Plastic Ideas' closing "Fiction Friction" delivers a killing blow.

Though the writing had started before they left for the Fake Train tour, the bulk of it was completed in the three months after.

Unwound was hitting its creative stride. They were tight, they were inspired, and they were evolving. Fast.
Many great punk rock records thrive on feeling claustrophobic. New Plastic Ideas roams free in an open field.
What Unwound's debut LP introduced, New Plastic Ideas refined.

At times even more aggressive—the off-kilter, vertiginous rhythm of "Entirely Different Matters"; the neck-snapping velocity of "What Was Wound"; the relentless pounding at the end of "All Souls Day"—New Plastic Ideas was also less recognizable as punk or hardcore.
Lund and bassist Vern Ramsey lean into ever-more-creative time signatures, laying the groundwork of Unwound's mathy edge to the usual breathless pace.
On New Plastic Ideas, it was going, oh, we're going to do a bunch of open-E stuff with harmonics, and then we're going to triple-track it, and it'll be this big, slowly evolving, pentatonic roar

Although Justin and Vern had written songs in drop D with Brandt and for Fake Train, they consider "Arboretum," one of the signature tracks on New Plastic Ideas, their first real foray into non-standard tunings.

"We tuned up just a half-step, to F, so the barre chords would be bigger," Justin explained. "But the cool thing about alternate tunings is they're all based on open tunings, so certain strings ring out. of What

Released 1995

  • Justin Trosper

  • Vern Rumsey

From the outset, The Future of What swiftly identifies itself as Unwound's heaviest work to date - a challenge to rock that never holds back.

The album's opener, "New Energy" explodes with waves of dissonance cornered by angular riffs and drums. Songs such as "Equally Stupid" come to be defined by odd time signatures that leave the listener off-balance, grasping for a semblance of familiarity to hold onto for dear life. Others, like "Pedals Like Bricks" arrives with a wall of noise that hits that same listener with like a brick.

"Here Comes The Dogs" adopting the opening riff on Nirvana's "Smells Like Team Spirit" before dropping a bassline with an attitude throws away any and all expectations - a defiant rejection of the oversaturation of grunge.

The Future of What isn't just lyrically depressing. Justin Trosper on "Disappoint" steps beyond the bound of language to deliver a guitar drenched in emotion, closing on the song on a riff that pleads, isolated and abandoned.

Artistic ambition aside, The Future of What simply fucking rips.
These songs don't just roar; they brawl, bruise, and on "Here Come The Dogs," one of the most deliriously vicious songs the band ever recorded, they downright maul.
To me it was sort of this anti-grunge thing, like, these are ideas we should be thinking about. The popular alternative stuff was just the most boring aspects of underground music. The Butthole Surfers making watered-down rock records. Those elements like post-punk and no wave, I was definitely pushing us toward stuff like that.

Repetition

Released 1996

  • Justin Trosper

  • Vern Rumsey

  • Sara Lund

It doesn't take long to realize that 1996's Repetition is defined by Vern Rumsey who makes it immediately clear that he owns this album. With their bassist at the forefront, Unwound takes its brand of moody post-hardcore to a tighter and groovier place than ever witnessed prior.

Opening tracks "Message Received," "Corpse Pose," and "Unauthorized Autobiography" make it no illusion how refined Trosper and Rumsey's guitar interplay has become since the band's inception.

The record also serves as the petri dish for Unwound's noise experimentation, with shrill, shrieking guitars interpliced between the mysterious verses on "Lowest Common Denominator" and driving rythmn of "Fingernails on a Chalkboard". The latter song, making expert use of a clave-esque drum beat, sends the listener down an anxious spiral, catalyzed by a seemingly unresolved 5/4 time signature.

"Go to Dallas and Take a Left" ends in free jazz pandemonium, foreshadowing the ambition running through Unwound's to-be 1998 LP Challenge For a Civilized Society.

"Lady Elect" stands as a solumn tribute to a friend of Justin Trosper who committed suicide before the recording of the album, carrying all the emotional weight of a man crippled by loss.

Repetition was Unwound's biggest commercial success during the band's lifetime. It sold fewer than 20,000 copies.
Unwound were never going to be a huge band, but Repetition was their best shot at becoming one; it's their cleanest, meatiest, most polished record, with guitars that mirror the sleek, lean contours of a foreign car.

Society

Released 1998

  • Justin Trosper

Challenge For a Civilized Society is exactly that - a challenge - a demonstration that Unwound couldn't be bound by a genre or label.

The 1998 LP expels any and all expectations, defined by spacious textures, immersive builds, and infectious riffs, the latter especially present on the head-turning "Laugh Track".

All of this comes to a head on the album's 7th track, "Side Effects of Being Tired" - a song can only be defined by entropy. The track opens with a driving bassline and dissonant guitar, accompanied by resounding horns, exploding into a free-jazz cacophony with a dying saxophone at the helm. Suddenly, the listener is thrust into waves of dissonance, guided down a dizzying psychedelic spiral that dissolves to a tearing sea of white noise, rising out the other side reversed before fading to silence.

A horrifingly fitting closer, a 2 minute untitled track finishes out the album with the existential dread of being lost in space.

"Side Effects of Being Tired" (featuring vocals and lyrics by Rumsey) ferries through both a free-jazz freakout and extended ambient noise coda in one of the boldest, most challenging tracks Unwound laid to tape.

On stage, we combined punishment and transcendence.

But we could also transport an entire room, taking an epic journey, bringing along anyone left standing.

If Unwound's discography was the five stages of grief, then Leaves Turn Inside You would be acceptance.

Emerging from a homemade studio made out of a barn, the 2001 LP sees Unwound take steps into the winter cold, equally bitter and beautiful. Clocking in at a hefty 74 minutes, the Tumwater trio embarks on a journey unlike anything released before or since.

With the chilling guitar riffs of "October All Over", the cathartic bridge on "December", and the mind-bending, dreamy, atmospheric textures throughout "One Lick Less", Unwound abandons their post-hardcore roots, setting themselves to explore their sound - no strings attached.

Suddenly, the band delivers "Terminus" - a track of three worlds - ensuring a false sense of security before the band makes their best chello-backed impression of Godspeed You! Black Emperor, then switching to one of the most beautiful instrumental codas ever laid to tape.

At track 13, Unwound reveals "Below the Salt," a dreary ten minute behemoth that observes Unwound - with a piano in their arsenal - build to one final climax, tying a fittingly solemn bow to Unwound's decade-long career.

"Vern and I had this conversation when we were getting involved with BMG about having a plan in case things got weird," Justin recalled. “It was a drunken conspiracy that I don't think Sara was privy to. We called it our five-year plan or something, and we decided that we would just break up the band in five years no matter what."

Albeit unintentionally, that's exactly what Unwound did. But not before recording their masterpiece.

Vern Rumsey

There's something especially satisfying about late-blooming bands who spend their whole career building up to their best music, and then calling it a day.

That's basically Unwound's arc in a nutshell; the Olympia trio spent a decade gradually tweaking and refining their post-hardcore until they achieved genuine transcendence on their monumental swan song, 2001's Leaves Turn Inside You.

They went out on a glorious high that few bands will ever match.
Hardcore punk prioritizes brevity, aggression, and heart-on-sleeve bloodletting, all of which are exact antonyms of descriptions you'd give to Unwound's last album.
That dreary ambiance bleeds into Leaves Turn Inside You, which is huge and haunted like nothing else in their discography, conjuring the crescendo-ing sweep of post-rock and the ominous scent of late-autumn rot.
[Leaves Turn Inside You] was a culmination of ideas we started while recording Challenge of a Civilized Society.

It was supposed to be psychedelic without being "psychedelic."

The whole lyrical approach was derived from things I wrote in dream journals.

We had never invited other players to play with us. But with Leaves Turn Inside You, there were so many other musical elements that couldn't be pulled off by just the three of us, which is why Dave Stone and Brandt Sandeno were invited to join us to help recreate the album in a live setting.
I remember one point where something kind of screwy was going on with the van and we were standing outside of it and Justin kinda turned to me with this patented shit-eating grin and he was chuckling.

I asked him, "What?" Justin just said "This is the way we do things," like, "We are fucking idiots."
I remember playing a really good show in Atlanta. We did an extra set of punk covers with Mecca Normal and The Thrones.

It wasn't all ages, but there were a bunch of kids freaked out from the past few weeks trying to get in. Unwound usually played all-ages events, but for some reason It didn't work out for that show. So I opened the side stage door for them, and they rushed in.

Years later, Bradford Cox from Deerhunter came up to me and told me he was one of those kids I helped get in. He remembered everything from that night... the covers we did, what amps, pedals and guitars I played!
Sara and I had always had kind of an abrasive relationship, like brother and sister, so it was easy for us to hate/love each other, but once the music started I loved her.

She is the best drummer I know of. I could look at her and read her mind, I think that she felt the same way.
[The September 11 and 12 shows in Boston and New York] were both cancelled. They were also our two biggest shows of the whole tour.

That certainly didn't help make it suck any less.
Traveling around was really surreal for sure. I guess it was difficult to play music in the face of the weird emotional atmosphere everywhere. Like, should we really be doing this? I now think that, of course, we should have.

People tend to continue doing the "normal" things they do even if everything around them is fucked up. That's how you survive bad stuff.

It's not like you are ignoring the bad situation; you are just acknowledging it and not letting it rule all your decisions that day.
I'm glad that we kept on—and as dysfunctional as it was—people needed an outlet, not just us, but also the people who came to see us.

People needed to let out feelings, be it aggression or sorrow. It was an emotional time for all of us.
I remember sitting in the studio with Justin working on a song and telling him that my girlfriend was pregnant.

I kind of felt like that was the beginning of the end.
I think that Justin and Sara put it best. I was stressed, drunk, and high, and I had a new little lady [daughter Lola] that I wanted to be with, so I didn't pull my weight on that tour like I had in the past.

Usually I was right on top of it, but I fell apart before, during, and after that tour. It was a terrible thing that I couldn't keep it together, but I just couldn't. I lost my strength.

Unfortunately, because I still feel like we could have put out more music and toured more if I could have kept my shit together.
Vern's personal crisis was screaming at all of us in the face and we just kept turning away, waiting for things to fix themselves or something.

We were young and really not equipped to deal with the demons that were staring us in the face.
Basically, what happened is that Vern dealt with the stress of everything by self-medicating with alcohol, and at our show in San Diego he was unable to really play due to intoxication.

After the show, he broke one of his hands by punching the wall backstage.

We had two more shows to play and we sent him home after the next show because it was pointless. His hand was basically a lobster claw. It was craziness, and it was over.
I think [Vern] actually broke both of his hands—both of his pinkies. We played one show with his broken hands and he was in total agony, of course.

We were pretty pissed at him, so it was hard to feel sympathy. But then again, it was such a sad and desperate act...
To be clear, Vern is not and has never been an asshole.

He is a very sweet, charming, and unbelievably likeable guy. Not to mention the best goddamned bass player ever.

Our anger and frustration came from his self-destruction and our feeling of total impotence in getting him to stop. We were completely unequipped for this level of damage.
It wasn't the performances of the songs on any night that made me feel that Unwound would or should break up... not 9/11, losing interest in music or how much everyone loved (and loves) each other. The feedback from longtime friends and fans was positive. Guarantees were up. Major labels were interested.

I have no doubt that Unwound would have had the same success that Modest Mouse has if alcoholism hadn't dimmed all that is bright.
Unwound were (and still are) beautiful people. Unwound was one of the most influential and vital bands of the '90s, and their last record was a masterpiece.

You just don't know it yet.

Unwound's influence will live on for a long time to come, and they were everything a band should be: independent and non-compromising in every aspect.
The band hasn't released a note of music in the past two decades and never will again. But I still hear Unwound damn near everywhere I go.
